UU — Engineering Journey

A first-year ISE student's palm-sized gecko quadruped. Selected lessons from months of solo development — a record of mistakes that taught me how to think about robots.

Lesson 01

When your bio-inspiration is wrong, your mechanics can't save you

My initial plan was cable-driven legs. I built out the mechanical design: threading holes, foot-end cable clamps, and routing sheaths. At palm-size, FDM print tolerances made the threading holes inconsistent — a single pull could torque a joint backwards.

But the deeper problem was conceptual. Vertebrate quadruped legs have elbows, meaning adjacent joints bend in opposite directions. I was designing a vertebrate with an arthropod's driver.

TAKEAWAY: The choice of biological reference is a load-bearing design decision. Get it wrong and no amount of mechanical cleverness recovers it.
Lesson 02

Don't place the motor where the pivot should be; place the pivot where the motor allows

I copied a design that used a four-bar linkage plus a cam for roll-axis lift. But the cam footprint was too large. I realized pitch DOF is non-negotiable for UU because uneven terrain requires the hip to move fore-aft.

The fix was a geometric pivot: the foot-end trajectory doesn't need to orbit the motor shaft directly — it just needs the shank's highest point to stay above the motor output. Once I let the effective pivot float, the arch trajectory fell out of the math.

TAKEAWAY: The motor's location and the effective rotational center don't have to be the same point. This unlocks geometries that look impossible.
Lesson 03

Mirrored in space, inverted in time

I mirrored the front and rear legs as exact geometric copies. But UU is independently controlled per leg. When I tried to walk with geometric symmetry, the rear legs couldn't produce forward thrust.

The fix was temporal. The four-bar state of the rear leg during its stance phase needs to correspond to the front leg's swing phase state — the mirror is in time, not in shape.

TAKEAWAY: Geometric symmetry doesn't imply temporal symmetry. What looks mirrored in CAD may need to be phase-inverted in firmware.

// SMALL BATTLE SCARS

  • Power Rails: Servos need an independent rail.MCU regulators produce voltage sag and twitching.
  • PWM Limits: ESP32-C3 has limited PWM channels; solved by time-multiplexing with attach()/detach().
  • CAD vs Print: Designed with explicit +0.2mm clearance on mating features after re-drilling every hole.
  • Over-engineering: Packed too many stiffening features in 4cm² and couldn't get a screwdriver in.
